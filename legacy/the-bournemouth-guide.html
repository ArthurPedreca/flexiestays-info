About Bournemouth

What is Bournemouth?

Bournemouth is a coastal resort town in Dorset, on the south coast of England, best known for seven miles of sandy beach, its Victorian pier and gardens, and one of the mildest climates in the country.

Founded in 1810 as a health retreat, the town grew into one of the UK's most visited seaside destinations. Today it pairs a classic beach holiday pier, promenade, beach huts, gently shelving sand with a modern food scene, a full events calendar and easy access to the Jurassic Coast World Heritage Site, the New Forest and Poole Harbour.

This guide is built by the team behind bournemouth.co.uk, the town's official tourism site, so every recommendation comes from people who live and work here.

Top attractions in Bournemouth

The six places that define a first visit all within, or a short ride from, the town centre.

01 Bournemouth Pier

Landmark · Seafront

The town's Victorian pier, first opened in 1880, now with a zip line to the beach, amusements and views along the full sweep of Poole Bay.

02 Lower Gardens

Gardens · Free entry

Landscaped Victorian gardens running from the town centre to the seafront along the Bourne stream home to mini golf, an aviary and summer live music.

03 Oceanarium

Family · Seafront

An aquarium on the promenade taking you from Poole Bay to the Amazon, Antarctica and the Great Barrier Reef. A reliable rainy-day plan.

04 Russell-Cotes Art Gallery & Museum

Culture · Clifftop

A Victorian villa above the pier packed with art and treasures collected on world tours one of the most distinctive small museums in England.

05 Hengistbury Head

Nature reserve · Coastal walk

A headland nature reserve at the eastern end of the beach with views to the Isle of Wight and the Purbecks, plus the land train to Mudeford Sandbank.

06 Boscombe Pier & Overcliff

Surf · Independent cafés

Bournemouth's second pier anchors the town's watersports scene, with surf schools, paddleboard hire and a growing café strip on the overcliff.

Neighbourhoods

Bournemouth, area by area

Each part of town has its own pace. Knowing the difference is the fastest way to plan a good trip.

The centre

Town Centre & The Triangle

The Square, the Lower Gardens, the pier and the main shopping streets plus The Triangle, the town's independent quarter for coffee, bars and live venues.

West

Westbourne

A village-style suburb of Victorian arcades, delis and independent shops, ten minutes from Alum Chine beach. The best brunch density in town.

East

Boscombe

Bournemouth's creative quarter: a restored pier, surf culture, vintage shops and an emerging food scene along Christchurch Road.

Far east

Southbourne

Laid-back and local, with a walkable high street, quieter sands and direct access to Hengistbury Head's coastal trails.

What's On

A town that changes with the seasons

Bournemouth runs a full calendar of festivals, sport and live events. These are the headline moments of the year.

Spring

Bay Run & Bournemouth 7s

The Vitality Bournemouth Bay Run takes over the seafront, followed by the Bournemouth 7s sport and music festival over the May bank holiday.

Summer

Peak beach season

Lifeguarded beaches, outdoor cinema, live music in the Lower Gardens and the busiest events programme of the year.

Autumn

Arts & food

Exhibitions, theatre seasons and food events as the crowds thin the best time for coastal walks to Hengistbury Head.

Winter

Christmas in Bournemouth

The Christmas Tree Wonderland and festive markets light up the town centre and gardens through December.

Plan Your Visit

Getting here is the easy part

By train

Direct services from London Waterloo take around 2 hours. Bournemouth station is a 20-minute walk or short bus ride from the beach.

By car

Roughly 2–2.5 hours from London via the M3, M27 and A338. Seafront and town-centre car parks fill early on summer weekends arrive before 10am.

By air & coach

Bournemouth Airport connects the town with European destinations, and National Express coaches run direct from London and major UK cities.

Common Questions

Bournemouth, answered

What is Bournemouth known for?

Seven miles of sandy beaches, its Victorian pier and gardens, one of the mildest climates in the UK, a lively food and nightlife scene, and major events year-round. It sits on the Dorset coast, next to the Jurassic Coast World Heritage Site.

How do I get to Bournemouth from London?

Direct trains from London Waterloo take around 2 hours. By car it's roughly 2–2.5 hours via the M3 and M27. National Express coaches run direct, and Bournemouth Airport serves European routes.

Which is the best beach in Bournemouth?

Bournemouth Beach by the pier is the most popular and best equipped. Locals favour Alum Chine and Southbourne for quieter days, while Boscombe is the pick for surf and watersports. All five sit on the same seven-mile stretch of sand.

Is Bournemouth beach sandy or pebbly?

Sandy the entire seven-mile stretch from Alum Chine to Southbourne, making it one of the longest continuous sandy beaches in the UK.

When is the best time to visit Bournemouth?

June to September for the warmest weather and fullest events calendar; May and late September for milder crowds. Thanks to the town's microclimate, the beaches and coastal walks are worth visiting all year.

Is Bournemouth good for families?

Yes gently shelving sandy beaches with seasonal lifeguard cover, the Oceanarium, pier amusements, parks and gardens, and a wide choice of family-friendly places to stay.
